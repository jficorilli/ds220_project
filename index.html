Jason Ficorilli DS 220 Project 2 - Exploring Pennsylvania's 2022 Motor Vehicle Crash Data

Introduction:

Driving is an essential aspect of daily life for many residents in the state of Pennsylvania. According to the Pennsylvania Department of Transportation (PennDOT), 85.6% of Pennsylvania county residents over the age of 16 on average hold a drivers license [1]. However, while motor vehicles provide many benefits opposed to other means of transportation, vehicular crashes are a serious concern for statewide transportation logistics and public safety. Therefore, information and insights gathered from motor vehicle crash data can be beneficial to many relevant personal, business, and government stakeholders looking to improve the quality of transportation safety in the state.

In this project, I utilized various data analysis packages in Python to analyze crash data from the state of Pennsylvania. I used data from the online PennDOT Crash Information Tool to evaluate relevant trends in motor vehicle crashes during the year 2022 [2]. The 2022 statewide dataset is the most recent publically-avaiable crash dataset for Pennsylvania and contains 100 columns of information related to the location, type of vechile(s) involved, and injury/fatality counts (among many other column types) for reportable crash incidents. According to PennDOT, a "reportable crash" is a crash where a vehicle received damage to the degree that it can not be driven away under its own power and therefore must be towed, or any injury or death occurs to any person involved [3]. The dataset therefore does not contain information about small or non-reportable accidents (i.e. "fender benders").

This project consisted of two parts:

  1. Reading in and cleaning the crash dataset
  2. Analyzing relevant questions about the dataset using tools from the Pandas and Matplotlib data anlaysis packages in Python.

The questions I evaluated are as follows:

  1. What were the top 10 PA counties with the most reportable crashes in 2022?
  2. What were the top 10 PA counties with the most reportable crashes per capita in 2022?
  3. Which months had the most crashes?
  4. Which day of the week had the most crashes?
  5. What were the top 10 hours of the day that had the most crashes?
  6. Of crashes for drivers aged 16-20, 21-49, 50-64, 65-74, and 75+, which age group had the most drivers involved?
  7. Of crashes for drivers aged 16, 17, 18, and 20 which age group had the most drivers involved?

This statement revealed that the values for nearly every column, whether a cateogrical or a numerical label or not, consisted of integer values. This is a result of the organization described in the aforementioned "data dictionary" created by PennDOT for this data, as the integer values for categorical columns correspond to a categorical value (such as a CRASH_MONTH value "1" corresponding to "January") [3]. To address this, I created dictionaries for each categorical column of interest, with the integer values as keys to their corresponding string (as defined in the PennDOT data dictionary). Next, I looped over the columns of the dataframe to change the types of the columns of interest from integers to strings. I then used the "replace()" function to map the values of each integer to its string value in the dictionary for the corresponding value (where "inplace=True" fully changes the value). The first entry in the cleaned dataset is shown below to confirm these changes, along with the new types of variables.

Discussion of Findings and Conclusion

This project has evaluated various trends present in the dataset for reportable crashes in Pennsylvania during 2022. While each question addressed in the project evaluated a different aspect of the data related to crashes, the observations paint a larger picture of different and potentially underlooked factors that could contribute to crashes. This particularly applies to the questions addressing the times of day and months of the year with the most crashes, as the "rush-hour" period and late fall/early winter months both held the highest counts of crashes. These findings could inform state and county lawmakers to reconsider how they distribute first-responder-services during the hours of the day and months of the year to better accomodate for periods of high crash frequency.

Further, the results from the analysis of age groups could encourage local and state lawmakers to continue allowing older drivers to operate motor vehicles (opposed to instituting any kind of widespread later "re-test" or age limit on driving). Currently, according to a PennDOT "Pennsylvania Senior Citizens Non-Commercial Driver's Licennse and Vechicle Registration Services" fact sheet, PennDOT randomly selects 1,900 drivers over the age of 45 each month to retest for their driver's license seven months prior to their license renewal date [5]. While the age group results from my analysis must be considered relative to each age group's total drivers in PA (data that was not available in the crash dataset), the similar distrubutions of crashes for drivers under the age of 21 and the greater amount of crashes for drivers aged 16-20 than those aged 65 or older could support maintaining the low amount of drivers randomly selected for a driving re-test (relative to the total amount of drivers in the state).

Lastly, the graph information resulting from the raw county crash counts and rate per capita could be relevant for lawmakers in many different PA counties. In particular, officials in Allegheny County could use the information that their county has the highest crash rate of any other county in the state (including Philadelphia County, a county that has more 350,000 people in its population) to dedicate more resources towards crash prevention in the county. Additionally, lawmakers in Fulton County, the county with the highest per capita reportable crash rate in the state, could also direct more resources towards their crash prevention legislation.
